I am a planetary scientist and astronomer researching planetary atmospheres and their interactions with charged particles and magnetic fields. My work to date has primarily involved observing and interpreting ultraviolet, visible and near-infrared spectra of auroral and airglow emissions. I have collected and analyzed data using the HIRES instrument at the W. M. Keck Observatory on Maunakea, NIRSpec on the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) and the IUVS instrument on NASA's MAVEN mission to Mars. I am also active in the development of open-source software utilities for data reduction and visualization.

Galilean Satellite Aurora

The thin atmospheres of Jupiter's Galilean satellites (Io, Europa, Ganymede and Callisto) have aurora just like Earth's northern lights. We use the Keck telescopes on the summit of Maunakea to gather optical wavelength spectra of the satellites when they pass into Jupiter's shadow to learn about the composition of their atmospheres and the properties of the plasma particles exciting the emissions.

Martian Aurora and Nightglow

The nightside atmosphere of Mars glows due to both interaction with charged particles from the Sun (auroras) and chemical reactions taking place in its upper atmosphere (nightglow). Auroral phenomena reveal the structure of Mars's remnant crustal magnetic field and exhibit several distinct morphologies, appearing both globally during strong solar outbursts and at discrete spatial locations during quiescent periods. Nightglow phenomena reflect instantaneous reaction rates in the upper atmosphere and reveal downwelling from Mars's thermosphere, tracing vertical winds over time.
